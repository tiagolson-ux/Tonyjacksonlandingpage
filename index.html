Research Spotlight: Little TJ’s Journey & the School-to-Prison Pipeline

Tony Jackson’s work confronts how bias and disproportionality push Black boys into the school-to-prison pipeline. In his research poster, “Our Stories Matter, But Do They? Little TJ’s Journey, Bias, Disproportionality, and the School-to-Prison Pipeline”, he uses the story of “Little TJ” to show how early labels and discipline decisions can follow a student from kindergarten into adulthood.

The Narrative: Little TJ’s Story

  • Elementary School (K–5): Little TJ’s high energy is interpreted as “defiance,” leading to repeated behavior referrals instead of support.
  • Middle School (6–8): Academic challenges and isolation grow as he is placed in more restrictive settings and separated from peers and curriculum.
  • High School (9–12): Suspensions and contact with School Resource Officers increase, creating a direct path from the classroom to the justice system and limiting post-secondary options.

Critical Frameworks

Tony analyzes Little TJ’s experience through critical lenses that reveal:

  • How Black boys are often viewed as inherently disruptive or criminal rather than in need of support.
  • How disability labels and restrictive placements can be used as tools for exclusion from general education.
  • How structural racism in policies and practices produces and maintains disproportionate outcomes.

Why This Work Matters

  • 40% of students expelled each year are Black.
  • 70% of students referred to law enforcement or arrested at school are Black or Latinx.
  • Black students are 3.5× more likely to be suspended than White peers.
  • Black and Latinx students are 2× more likely not to graduate from high school.
  • 68% of people in state and federal prisons do not have a high school diploma.

Tony’s work calls educators and policymakers to move from bias to equity by transforming how we label students, design special education services, and use school discipline.
